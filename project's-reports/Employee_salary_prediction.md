Executive SummaryThis project outlines the end-to-end development of a machine learning workflow designed to analyze and predict employee monthly salaries based on demographic, corporate, and performance attributes. The analysis evolved from initial exploratory data cleaning to multi-model testing and feature engineering. By identifying key interaction terms, the model’s predictive capability was successfully doubled, shifting the explanatory power ($R^2$) from an initial 0.058 to 0.102.  2. To solve this, two engineered features were injected prior to data splitting:  Experience-to-Age Ratio (Exp_to_Age_Ratio): Captured career velocity by contextualizing experience against total age.  Performance-Experience Interaction (Perf_x_Exp): Multiplied performance scores by total experience, recognizing that high performance carries compounding salary weight over a longer career tenure.  This intervention successfully boosted the model's capacity to explain salary variance to 10.2%.  5. Primary Salary Drivers (Feature Importance)The final trained Random Forest model extracted the true underlying weights dictating how salaries are distributed within the company.  Age (20.9% importance): The single most dominant factor determining baseline salary scale.  Work Experience (18.1% importance): Seniority yields significant compounding value over time.  Performance Score (13.4% importance): High individual contribution serves as a major differentiator within equivalent roles.  Corporate & Demographics: Factors such as specific geographic city location, remote work status, and gender had nominal, minor weights compared to core merit and tenure metrics.  6. Strategic Recommendations for Future IterationsWhile feature engineering successfully moved the needle, an $R^2$ of 10.2% indicates that substantial variables affecting salary remain uncaptured. To scale this framework into a production-grade compensation engine, the following steps are recommended:  Incorporate Missing Variables: Supplement the dataset with structural features such as specific technical certifications, domain expertise (e.g., programming languages), and annual billable hours.Expand Sample Size: Increasing the record count beyond 200 rows will drastically stabilize the Random Forest model and reduce predictive noise.  Deploy Non-Linear Boosters: Migrate the pipeline from a standard Random Forest to advanced gradient-boosted frameworks such as XGBoost or LightGBM to map subtle interactions more aggressively.
